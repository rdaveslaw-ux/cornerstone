Conversational Diagnostic Guide
Forming Intentional Disciples — Integrated Framework
Training Resource
Experienced Men's Ministry Leaders
Purpose
This guide trains experienced men to correctly identify where another man is in the Integrated Framework through natural conversation — not a formal assessment. The goal is to read what a man reveals about himself in ordinary speech.
Core principle
Listen for how a man talks about his faith as much as what he says. Vocabulary, framing, emotional tone, and whether he speaks in first or third person are often more diagnostic than the content of his answers.
I
Part One
Five Diagnostic Questions
1
"Tell me about your relationship with God — what's it actually like right now?"
The master diagnostic. Opens everything. Let him answer without rescuing him from silence.
Listen for: Does he describe a relationship or a practice? Does he say "Jesus" personally or "God" abstractly? Does he speak in first person ("I talked to Him about...") or institutional third person ("The Church teaches...")?
2
"Was there ever a moment when your faith became personally yours — not just something you were handed?"
The conversion hinge. Separates Phase 1–2 from Phase 3 and above.
Listen for: Can he name a specific moment, season, or decision? Is the language personal and owned, or vague and deflecting? If he says "I've always believed," probe: "Has it always felt this personal to you?"
3
"What happened to your faith the last time your life got really hard?"
The soil test. Nothing reveals depth like what faith does under pressure.
Listen for: Was faith relevant to the hard time, or completely separate from it? Did it hold, deepen, or fade? "I got through it" is not an answer — push gently: "Did your relationship with God get closer or more distant during that?"
4
"What's the one thing that most gets between you and the man you know you're supposed to be?"
Reveals the dominant thorn, the happiness level, and how much honest self-awareness exists.
Listen for: Does he give a safe, surface answer — or name something real with specificity? Does he have a plan of attack (accountability, spiritual direction)? Or does he name it and shrug? Shame without accountability suggests Phase 2–3.
5
"Is there anyone walking alongside you in your faith right now — and is there anyone you're walking with?"
The fatherhood diagnostic. Always ask both directions: being mentored and mentoring.
Listen for: Does he name specific people, or speak in generalities? Does he see himself as someone others need, or only as someone who needs others? Phase 5 men instinctively ask this question back to you.
II
Part Two
Key Phrases & Signals by Phase
How to use this section: These are patterns to listen for, not boxes to force a man into. Most men will show signals across two adjacent phases — when that happens, place him in the lower one. Click or tap each phase to expand its signals. All phases print fully expanded.
Phase 1
Pre-Evangelization
Faith is cultural, inherited, or institutional — not personal
▾
Phrases you're likely to hear
  • I was raised Catholic
  • I go to Mass when I can
  • I believe in something
  • Religion is important to me / my family
  • I'm not really into organized religion but...
  • The Church teaches... / Catholics believe...
  • I'm not sure God is that personal
Behavioral & posture cues
  • Describes faith in third person or institutional terms
  • Never says "Jesus" personally — always "God" in the abstract
  • Can describe Catholic practice but not personal encounter
  • When asked about prayer, pivots to what he does, not who he knows
  • Changes subject or gets uncomfortable when faith gets personal
  • Faith and life are in separate compartments
Watch for: Men who are devout practitioners — daily Mass, rosary, active in parish — can still be in Phase 1 if the practice has never produced a personal encounter. Do not let the religious activity mislead you.
Phase 2
Evangelization
Something is stirring — questions forming, but faith not yet personally owned
▾
Phrases you're likely to hear
  • I've been thinking about...
  • Something happened and I started wondering...
  • I'm not sure what I believe anymore
  • There has to be more than this
  • I used to be more involved, but...
  • I had an experience that shook me
  • I'm kind of exploring right now
Behavioral & posture cues
  • Faith was activated by a crisis — birth, death, divorce, failure
  • Can describe spiritual questions but not spiritual answers
  • Curiosity is fragile — easily scared off by moralizing or boredom
  • Often has a specific person or event that opened the door
  • May be returning after a long absence from practice
  • Asks questions more than makes statements
Watch for: The man whose faith surged after a crisis, filled with enthusiasm — then faded when the crisis resolved. This is rocky-ground Phase 2, not Phase 3 conversion. The test is always: did it hold when the pressure was off?
Phase 3
Initial Conversion
Surrender has occurred — zeal and enthusiasm, but roots still shallow
▾
Phrases you're likely to hear
  • Since my retreat... / Since I went to confession...
  • I gave my life to Christ / I surrendered
  • God has been so good to me lately
  • I feel like a completely different person
  • I've been going to daily Mass / confession weekly
  • I never understood this before, but now...
  • I want to tell everyone
Behavioral & posture cues
  • New vocabulary that sometimes feels borrowed, not fully integrated
  • Speaks mostly about what God has done for him — rarely about cost
  • High enthusiasm, but no mention of accountability structure
  • Hasn't been seriously tested yet since the conversion
  • May be intense in a way that's still externalized (doing) not interior (being)
  • Can name the conversion; cannot yet name the ongoing formation
Watch for: Conversion zeal is the most common source of over-placement. A man who has surrendered and is on fire feels — and sounds — like Phase 4. The difference is testedness. Has his faith survived something hard since the surrender? If not, he belongs here.
Phase 4
Formation
Intentional disciple in active formation — tested, rooted, still growing
▾
Phrases you're likely to hear
  • My men's group... / My spiritual director...
  • I've been convicted about...
  • My faith was tested when... and it held
  • I've been wrong about this and had to change
  • We've been working through... [names a practice]
  • I struggle with [names something real and specific]
  • I've been praying about that for a while now
Behavioral & posture cues
  • Names specific spiritual practices he maintains consistently
  • Honest about ongoing sin and growth areas — not defensive or ashamed
  • References a real accountability relationship by name
  • Speaks about Scripture personally, not academically
  • Can describe where he was wrong and what changed his mind
  • Faith has clearly cost him something and he names it without heroics
Watch for: Parish leaders, small group facilitators, and theologically knowledgeable men may sound like Phase 4 while still operating from Phase 3 interior depth. The test is not what he knows or what role he holds — it is whether his faith has been refined by suffering and sustained by ongoing formation.
Phase 5
Mission & Fatherhood
Giving away what he has received — forming others, not just being formed
▾
Phrases you're likely to hear
  • The guy I'm walking with... [names him]
  • When I was in his shoes, I...
  • He's almost ready to lead his own group
  • I've been trying to get [name] to come to...
  • What I've learned after years of this is...
  • I don't know — but I know who does
  • I find my deepest joy in watching men change
Behavioral & posture cues
  • Speaks about faith primarily in terms of what others need to receive
  • Language of releasing: "He's ready..." / "I sent him to..."
  • Asks more questions than he answers — genuinely curious about you
  • Can name the men he has formed and where they are now
  • Says "I don't know" with confidence — has shed the need for answers
  • His spiritual conversation instinctively turns toward your formation
Watch for: This is the rarest placement. A man who leads a ministry, gives talks, or is widely respected can still be in Phase 4. The test: can he name specific men he is intentionally forming right now, and can he describe the fruit? If not, place him in Phase 4.
III
Part Three
Posture for the Conversation
Ask, don't diagnose
A man who senses he is being evaluated will perform. Keep the conversation exploratory and warm. Your placement happens after, in your own reflection.
Silence is data
How long a man pauses, whether he changes the subject, and what he won't say are as diagnostic as what he says. Don't fill the silence too fast.
Match his vocabulary, not yours
Reflect his language back to him. Don't introduce theological vocabulary he hasn't used — it signals inauthenticity and closes the conversation.
One question per meeting
These five questions are spread across multiple conversations, not a single interview. Each question deserves real time. Depth comes from frequency, not speed.
Default to the lower phase
When a man's signals span two phases, always place him in the lower. Underestimating depth means he receives challenge he can grow from. Overestimating it means he receives nothing he needs.
You are the pastoral response
The point of the placement is not a label — it is knowing what to offer this man next. The question after placement is always: what does he need from me, and who else should be in his life?
